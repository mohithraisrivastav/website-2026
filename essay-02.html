Why I Stopped Photographing Buildings

A quiet confession about the moment I realized the building was never the point.

8 min readMohith Rai Srivastav

There is a moment in every long-term project when the stated subject quietly stops being the actual subject. For me, with architectural photography, that moment arrived in late 2022, standing in the courtyard of a half-finished concrete house in Kochi at four in the afternoon, watching the shadows of a single coconut palm travel slowly across an unfinished wall.

I had been photographing weather. I had been photographing light. The building was simply the excuse — a surface against which everything else was made legible.

The architect draws the building. The weather finishes it. The photographer, at best, catches the sentence mid-clause.— Notebook, Kochi, 2022

The realisation was not dramatic. There was no single image that announced it. It came, as most structural realisations do, as a slow pressure against a previously stable assumption. I had assumed I was a photographer of architecture. What I had actually been doing, for years, was photographing the atmospheric conditions that architecture makes visible.

The confession

To say this aloud — in a practice built around architectural commissions, with clients who hired me precisely because they wanted their buildings documented — felt like a kind of professional betrayal. I said it anyway. First in my notebook, in the abbreviated language I use when I am working something out. Then to a colleague over coffee in a Bandra hotel lobby. Then, eventually, to a client who had trusted me with a three-year project and deserved an honest account of what he was actually getting.

He was quiet for a long moment. Then he said: I think I knew that. I think that is why I hired you.

This was either very reassuring or very unsettling. I have not yet decided which.

What changed

Once I said this aloud, everything in my practice quietly shifted. My pre-shoot site readings got longer — I began spending hours at a site before my first camera visit, not looking for angles but listening for the specific quality of light and sound the building accumulated at different times. My deliverable counts went down. My commissions got slower. My clients, to their considerable credit, largely followed.

I also stopped saying I was an architectural photographer in the way the phrase is usually deployed — as a signal of genre, of a certain kind of competence with wide-angle lenses and Interiors lit to a fare-thee-well. I started saying, less comfortably, that I was a photographer Interested in the way buildings hold weather and time. This description was harder to invoice. It was more accurate.

The building is one noun in a longer sentence. Weather is the verb. Time is the grammar. I photograph the sentence, not the noun.

What it costs

There are real costs to this position. Some clients want records. They want documentation. They want the building shown clean and correct and sufficiently lit, without the distractions of atmosphere or mood or the particular quality of a February morning at a site in South Goa. These clients and I are, as a rule, not a good match. We part politely.

Other clients want something else — something they often cannot name, but recognise immediately when they see it. These are the commissions I take. They are slower. They require more visits. The resulting images are not always the most technically correct. But they are the ones that, years later, still hold something that feels true to the experience of being in that building at that hour on that specific afternoon in October.

I still photograph buildings. I have never stopped. But I am no longer pretending that the building is the whole sentence. It is one noun in a longer grammar of weather, time, material, and the particular quality of attention that a photographer brings — or fails to bring — to a subject patient enough to wait.

The photographs I am most proud of are the ones where the building is almost incidental. Where the real subject is a shadow at the exact minute it crossed a threshold. A wall that was wet from a rain that stopped twenty minutes before I arrived. The residue of weather on a surface that an architect designed but did not author. Those are the photographs I am trying to make. I have been trying, in various ways, since 2022. I will, in all likelihood, be trying for the rest of my working life.

— M.R.S.

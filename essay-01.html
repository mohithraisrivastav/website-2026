On the Patience of Walls

What eighteen months inside a single laterite residence taught me about accumulation, surface, and the long, unhurried authorship of weather.

12 min readMohith Rai Srivastav

A brief studio note from the laterite house in Assagao — a practice of returning until the wall stopped being a subject and started being a collaborator.

The first time I photographed the house in Assagao, I got everything wrong. I arrived at the hour I thought was correct — mid-morning, when the light was neither rising nor tired — and I framed the laterite elevation as if it were a subject. I composed. I stepped back. I chose my focal length. I did all the things I had been trained to do. The photographs were competent, and I hated them.

The architect who had commissioned the work was polite. He said what clients say when they do not want to hurt you: Interesting. I booked a return visit three weeks later. Then another. Then another after that, until the return visits had become a practice, and the practice had become the project.

A wall does not pose. It accumulates. Your job is to stand there long enough to be noticed by what it has been accumulating.— Field Note, June 2023

What I eventually understood — and it took the full eighteen months to understand it — is that a laterite wall is not a surface so much as a timeline. Each rain leaves a residue. Each dry season subtracts it. Smoke from the kitchen on the west face. A child's chalk marks near the door. A lizard's thousand minute traversals. The architect drew the wall once; the weather redraws it continuously.

The wrong question

I had been asking the wrong question. I had been asking: how do I photograph this wall well? The better question turned out to be: when does the wall want to be photographed? And that question cannot be answered in advance. It can only be answered by returning.

Slowness as method

But slowness is not only a poetic position. It is also a methodological one. When you return to a wall ninety times, you do not simply see more frames. You see the wall differently. The wall acquires a temperament. It becomes, in an almost uncomfortable sense, a collaborator.

What the wall taught me

First: the wall is not the subject. The weather is the subject, and the wall is the instrument through which the weather records itself.

Second: duration is a kind of access. A wall photographed once shows you what it looks like. A wall photographed ninety times shows you what it is.

Duration is not patience. It is the fee you pay for the kind of access that cannot be scheduled.

Third: patience is not an aesthetic. It is a debt you pay to the subject.

The final sequence of the Assagao project is thirty-two frames. Ninety-seven visits produced those thirty-two. The ratio is, in commercial terms, grotesque. In photographic terms, it is approximately correct.

— M.R.S.
